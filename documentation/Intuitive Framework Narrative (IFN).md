The vehicle via which a [[Minimum Viable Framework (MVF)]] is first transmitted from the conceptual space to the mind of the reader. 

It acts as an intuitive explanation of the topic that seeks to provide that moment of sudden conceptual clarity one gets when a seemingly complex subject is finally explained well enough and to the perfect level of detail that it all makes sense now and is no longer intimidating. The main philosophy is that new information should not be memorized but rather be learned naturally and intuitively. 

One of the main aspects of the underlying philosophy is that rote memorization is a symptom of missing structure and is not a requirement for learning. This is a similar to [[Michel Thomas]]' language learning philosophy which basically seeks to expose the deep grammatical logic of a language such that sentences feel *inevitable* rather than recalled. The MFV/IFN philosophy is that the structural and functional logic of a system should be exposed first so that the facts and details feel like obvious follow-ups instead of disjointed pieces of information. In both cases the learner is not accumulating fragments but internalizing a backbone that constrains what can and cannot be true. Once the backbone is in place, mechanisms (or vocabulary) stop being things you *learn* and start being things you *recognize*. This works because in all complex systems (biological, linguistic, or other) the same patterns are repeated in various applications and if someone intuitively understands the logic behind them they don't even have to think about it in order to produce, which is the definition of ***[[fluency of knowledge]]***.

##### **Formal Criteria:**
- Sufficient anchoring sentence - single sentence must convey the systems purpose and scope
- Continuous narrative coherence - the text must form a single conceptual throughline, with each paragraph causally depending on the previous.
- Completeness - all essential MVF elements are implicitly present
- Necessity first - explanation must establish why something must exist before describing its mechanisms
- In-zooming - all statements must remain true and complete as technical resolution increases, later details should feel like zooming-in instead of additional concepts
- Predictive - after reading, the reader should be able to anticipate unseen details and make correct qualitative predictions
- Minimum cognitive load - the explanation must minimize the introduction of vastly new terms or anything else that requires memorization
- Direct language - the use of metaphors or analogies shall be minimized

##### **Standard Operating Procedure:**
**Input:** A validated MVF  
**Output:** An intuitive, chapter-style explanation

**Steps**
1. **Write the anchoring sentence**
	- Produce one standalone sentence that captures the system’s purpose and scope.
    - It must be understandable without prior context.
2. **Explain the core problem**
    - Describe the challenge the system exists to solve.
    - Emphasize why the problem is non-trivial or dangerous if unmanaged.
3. **Reveal necessity before detail**
    - Explain how the system solves the problem _in principle_.
    - Avoid naming mechanisms unless unavoidable.
4. **Justify structure**
    - Show why the system must be organized the way it is.
    - Tie structure directly to the problem and constraints.
5. **Expose key decisions or trade-offs**
    - Describe what the system must choose or balance.
    - Keep focus on consequences, not implementation.
6. **Connect to the larger system**
    - Explain how the system’s internal state affects other systems.
    - Emphasize signaling and integration.
7. **Describe failure intuitively**
    - Explain what goes wrong when control is lost and why.
    - Avoid technical pathology or edge cases.

**Style Constraints**
- Continuous prose (paragraphs, not sections).
- Plain, causal language.
- No metaphors, analogies, or exhaustive lists.
- Must remain valid as technical detail increases
